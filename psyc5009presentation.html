üìã Psychosis is a collection of symptoms that are characterized by a loss of contact with reality.

Psychosis is the hallmark of schizophrenia. It is characterized by a disconnect between what a person sees, hears, or feels and what is actually there. This can manifest in various ways:

Hear, see, or feel things that are not there.

Believing things that are not true.

Speaking in a way that is hard to understand.

Disorganized behavior means difficulty organizing one's actions or behaving inappropriately ‚Äì someone might neglect hygiene, wear bizarre outfits (like a winter coat in summer), or have unpredictable emotional outbursts.

Catatonic Symptoms

Catatonic symptoms are at the extreme end of disorganized behavior: the person might become very rigid, unresponsive, or hold strange postures for extended periods, or alternatively, exhibit purposeless excessive movement.

Phases

#1: Prodromal phase

The prodromal stage is characterized by subtle changes in behavior and mood. It is a time when the person is beginning to experience symptoms of schizophrenia, but they are not yet severe enough to be noticed, which makes it difficult to identify. Some symptoms include yet not limited to: social isolation, irritability, difficulty concentrating, and sleep disturbances.

#2 Active phase

This is the acute phase of schizophrenia, where the person experiences the core symptoms of psychosis such as hallucinations and delusions.

#3 Residual phase

Residual schizophrenia refers to a phase with reduced or less severe symptoms compared to the active (psychotic) phase. At this stage, the positive symptoms are typically absent. The person is likely to be experiencing negative symptoms, low motivation, low energy, depressed mood and so on. DSM-5-TR Update: The residual phase is no longer used for diagnostic purposes, but still useful clinically to describe symptom patterns in schizophrenia.

DSM-5 Criteria

To diagnose schizophrenia, the following criteria (A‚ÄìF) must be met:

üìù Quick Summary:

  • At least 2 characteristic symptoms (one must be hallucinations, delusions, or disorganized speech) for a significant portion of one month.
  • Functional decline in work, relationships, or self-care.
  • Continuous disturbance for at least 6 months, with at least 1 month of active symptoms.
  • Rule out mood disorders, substance use, and medical conditions.
A. Characteristic Symptoms

Two (or more) of the following, each present for a significant portion of time during a 1-month period (or less if successfully treated). At least one must be (1), (2), or (3):

  1. Delusions
  2. Hallucinations
  3. Disorganized speech (e.g., frequent derailment or incoherence)
  4. Grossly disorganized or catatonic behavior
  5. Negative symptoms (e.g., diminished emotional expression or avolition)
B. Social/Occupational Dysfunction

For a significant portion of the time since onset, level of functioning in one or more major areas such as work, interpersonal relations, or self-care is markedly below the level achieved prior to onset.

C. Duration

Continuous signs of the disturbance persist for at least 6 months. This 6-month period must include at least 1 month of symptoms that meet Criterion A (active-phase symptoms) and may include periods of prodromal or residual symptoms.

D. Schizoaffective and Mood Disorder Exclusion

Schizoaffective disorder and depressive or bipolar disorder with psychotic features have been ruled out because either:

  • No major depressive or manic episodes have occurred concurrently with the active-phase symptoms; or
  • If mood episodes have occurred during active-phase symptoms, they have been present for a minority of the total duration.
E. Substance/Medical Condition Exclusion

The disturbance is not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance or another medical condition.

F. Autism Spectrum Disorder Exclusion

If there is a history of autism spectrum disorder or a communication disorder of childhood onset, a diagnosis of schizophrenia is made only if prominent delusions or hallucinations are also present for at least 1 month (or less if successfully treated).

Diagnostic Challenges in Schizophrenia

No definitive lab tests or brain scans
  1. Schizophrenia is diagnosed clinically, based on observed symptoms and illness course.
  2. Diagnosis relies on interviews with the patient and family and careful observation over time.
Early-stage uncertainty
  1. In the first few weeks or months, it may be unclear whether the case is:
    • Schizophrenia
    • A brief psychotic episode
    • Mood-related psychosis
  2. Clinicians may delay diagnosis until the clinical picture becomes clearer.
Symptom Overlap with Other Disorders
  1. Severe depression
    • Can cause delusional guilt or even hallucinations.
  2. Bipolar mania
    • May include psychotic features such as grandiosity or hallucinations.
  3. Drug-induced psychosis
    • Methamphetamine use or other substance intoxication can mimic schizophrenia symptoms in the short term.
  4. Conclusion:
    • Diagnosing schizophrenia requires evaluating:
    • Symptom duration
    • Context (e.g., presence or absence of drug use)
    • Mood symptoms
  5. Clinicians must "piece together the puzzle" carefully.
Comorbid Conditions and Limited Insight
  • Comorbidities are common
    • Anxiety disorders
    • Depression
    • Substance abuse
  • These can muddy the clinical picture, making diagnosis more complex.
  • Limited insight
    • Patients may not report hallucinations or delusions, especially early on, due to:
      • Fear
      • Paranoia
      • Distrust
    • Families may only notice a person has "changed", without understanding the cause.
Diagnostic Delay and Its Causes
  • Diagnosis often occurs months or even a year or more after symptom onset.
  • Contributing factors:
    • Diagnostic complexity
    • Social stigma, which may lead families to avoid seeking psychiatric help
The Diagnostic Process
  • Mental health professionals are trained to:
    • Recognize schizophrenia symptom patterns
    • Conduct thorough interviews
    • Use psychological tests or standardized questionnaires when needed
  • A clear diagnosis helps shape an appropriate treatment plan.
Schizophrenia as a Spectrum Disorder
  • Schizophrenia is not all-or-nothing; it exists on a spectrum.
  • Variability in symptom patterns and outcomes:
    • Some individuals have severe, chronic symptoms
    • Others may:
      • Have one or two episodes then recover
      • Show mainly negative symptoms (e.g., lack of motivation) with few positive symptoms
  • Hence, the term "schizophrenia spectrum disorders" is often used.
Hope and Recovery
  • Outcomes vary, even for those with full schizophrenia diagnoses.
  • With support and treatment:
    • Many people can live fulfilling lives.
    • Success stories exist but are underrepresented in media.
  • Important reminder:
    • A diagnosis of schizophrenia is serious, but not the end of one's aspirations.

Medications

üí£ Myth Busters

Myth 8 Busted: Schizophrenia CAN be treated.

While there's no cure for schizophrenia yet, effective treatments are available in helping patients manage symptoms, reduce relapses, and improve functioning. Early identification and intervention may prevent or delay schizophrenia development and improve overall quality of life.

Medication Types

First-Gen Antipsychotics

Note.Chlorpromazine From Living with Schizophrenia UK [Online image], by Unknown, 2014 (Link)

Mechanism: Primarily block dopamine D2 receptors.

Examples: Haloperidol, Chlorpromazine.

Effectiveness: Strong for positive symptoms.

Side Effects: High risk of EPS, sedation, tardive dyskinesia.

(Source: bap.org.uk)

Second-Gen Antipsychotics

Note. Risperdal From News API [Online image], by Unknown, n.d. (Link)

Mechanism: Block D2 and 5-HT2A receptors.

Examples: Risperidone, Olanzapine, Quetiapine.

Effectiveness: As effective as FGAs for positive symptoms, better tolerability.

Side Effects: Weight gain, metabolic syndrome, sedation.

(Source: bap.org.uk)

Clozapine

Note. Clozapine From Google Images [Online image], by Unknown, n.d. (Link)

Mechanism: Modulates serotonin, dopamine, and others.

Effectiveness: Most effective for treatment-resistant schizophrenia, reduces suicide risk.

Side Effects: Agranulocytosis (requires monitoring), weight gain, seizures, sedation.

(Source: psychiatryonline.org)

New in 2024

KarXT (Cobenfy)

Note. KarXT (Cobenfy) From LinkedIn [Online image], by Unknown, 2024 (Link)

Mechanism: Targets muscarinic acetylcholine receptors.

Effectiveness: Significant reduction in positive symptoms, promising fewer side effects.

Side Effects: Early trials suggest fewer sedation, weight gain, or motor issues.

(Source: fda.gov, npr.org)

Summary

There is no one-size-fits-all solution ‚Äî effective care requires a multi-disciplinary, personalized approach involving medication, therapy, community support, and patient involvement.

Truth: Schizophrenia affects about 0.3%‚Äì0.7% of the population worldwide, with approximately 1 in 300 people globally and 1 in 100 in Australia living with the condition.

Truth: Schizophrenia is not the same as dissociative identity disorder (DID). It is a complex mental health condition characterized by disruptions in thinking, perception, and behavior.

Truth: Schizophrenia is a complex condition with multiple causes including genetic, biological, and environmental factors. Parenting style is not a cause.

Truth: While there is a genetic component, having a parent with schizophrenia does not guarantee you will develop it. The risk is higher but still relatively low.

Truth: Most people with schizophrenia are not violent. They are more likely to be victims of violence than perpetrators.

Truth: Intelligence is not affected by schizophrenia. Many people with schizophrenia have average or above-average intelligence.

Truth: Symptoms vary widely between individuals. Some may experience hallucinations and delusions, while others may have different symptoms like social withdrawal or cognitive difficulties.

Truth: While there is no cure, schizophrenia can be effectively treated with a combination of medication, therapy, and support services, allowing many people to lead fulfilling lives.

Hope, Challenges, Needs and Directions

There is no one-size-fits-all solution ‚Äî effective care requires a multi-disciplinary, personalized approach involving medication, therapy, community support, and patient involvement.

  • Symptom Control and Stability: Most individuals with schizophrenia can experience significant symptom relief‚Äîespecially of positive symptoms like hallucinations and delusions‚Äîthrough antipsychotic medication.
  • New Drug Development: The 2024 FDA approval of KarXT (Cobenfy) marks a major breakthrough. Unlike traditional antipsychotics, KarXT does not primarily block dopamine receptors and has shown fewer side effects like sedation and weight gain.
  • Early Intervention Works: Specialized early psychosis programs can dramatically improve long-term outcomes if treatment is initiated early, especially in youth.
  • Recovery-Oriented Care: The modern emphasis on holistic, person-centered approaches‚Äîincluding peer support and vocational rehab‚Äîoffers hope for meaningful, independent lives, even if some symptoms persist.

(Sources: bap.org.uk, fda.gov, npr.org, emedicine.medscape.com)

  • Adherence Issues: Medication non-adherence remains a critical issue, often due to side effects or lack of insight into the illness. Long-acting injectables can help, but uptake varies.
  • Treatment-Resistant Cases: About 25% of patients do not respond to standard antipsychotics. Clozapine is the best option in these cases but carries serious risks like agranulocytosis, requiring strict monitoring.
  • Side Effects and Metabolic Issues: Many antipsychotics, especially second-generation ones, cause significant weight gain and metabolic syndrome, increasing the risk of diabetes and heart disease.
  • Negative and Cognitive Symptoms: Current treatments are less effective for negative symptoms (e.g., social withdrawal, lack of motivation) and cognitive deficits. These symptoms often impair real-world functioning even when psychosis is controlled.
  • Relapse Risk: Schizophrenia has a relapsing-remitting course. Each relapse can worsen long-term outcomes. Preventing them requires continuous care and early detection of warning signs.
  • Stigma: Stigma remains a major barrier throughout treatment, leading to non-adherence, social withdrawal, or shame. It reduces engagement with care, making education, empathy, and peer support essential to counter hopelessness and promote recovery.

(Sources: bap.org.uk, psychiatryonline.org)

  • Better Tolerated Medications: There's a strong need for antipsychotics with fewer motor, cognitive, and metabolic side effects to improve adherence and long-term health outcomes.
  • Targeting Negative Symptoms and Cognition: Existing drugs are largely ineffective for the most disabling aspects of the disorder. New treatments must address this gap to improve quality of life and functional recovery.
  • Accessible Early Intervention Services: Programs for first-episode psychosis are not yet widely available, particularly in rural or underfunded areas, despite their proven efficacy.
  • Culturally Sensitive, Integrated Care: Tailored treatment that respects cultural differences, includes family, and provides coordinated care (e.g., through Assertive Community Treatment teams) is essential but inconsistently applied.
  • Non-Dopamine-Based Therapies: KarXT's success shows promise for non-dopaminergic pathways‚Äîlike the muscarinic acetylcholine system. Other targets being explored include glutamate modulation and inflammation pathways.
  • Digital and Tech-Enabled Care: Tools like smartphone apps for symptom monitoring and early relapse detection, and virtual reality for social skills training, are being developed to support engagement and autonomy.
  • Cognitive Remediation & Lifestyle Integration: Combining therapies that enhance memory and attention with lifestyle interventions (e.g., diet, exercise, smoking cessation) may improve both mental and physical health outcomes.
  • Recovery Model Integration: There's a growing emphasis on shifting away from a purely clinical focus to one that enables individuals to pursue education, work, relationships, and creative goals, even with lingering symptoms.

(Sources: fda.gov, npr.org)

So what can we do?

Researchers must keep pushing for answers.

Policy makers must expand access, early intervention, and equity in mental health.

Society must fight stigma and foster inclusion everywhere.

Each of us can listen, learn, and lead with empathy.
